🎨 Art Direction · 4 explorations

Same game. Four worlds it could live in.

You were right — the first pass borrowed the brief's night-jungle-and-emoji skin. This is a clean reset. Each world below renders the exact same Fart Back scene — a monkey farting a banana at a kid — so the only thing changing is the look and feel. None of them are emoji. None of them are the brief.

What the research says

A distinct artistic identity is what separates memorable games from forgettable ones.

Beat the cliché

Drop literal references (jungle, emoji). Read "playful" as bold composition + unexpected colour, not props.

Colour discipline

60 / 30 / 10 — one calm ground, one hero hue, one accent. Swap pure black for a tinted dark.

Comedy lineage

Fart humour has pedigree — WarioWare, Boogerman, Banjo-Kazooie. Lean into the absurd.

Gameplay stays identical across all four — dodge the farted bananas, blast them back, stun monkeys for combos. Art direction is a skin over the same mechanics, so we can commit to a world without touching the design.

My steer

If the goal is funniest, fastest, most addictive → Loud! — it's cheap to build, reads instantly on a phone, and its whole personality is comedy. If the goal is a keepsake made for Mateo → Doodle, which is the most personal and the biggest departure from anything generic. Inkwell and Plasticine are the "wow, this looks like a real studio made it" options — more effort, more payoff.

Whichever you pick, the next move is the same: I re-skin the working Fart Back prototype into that world — new palette, characters, textures, type, and a matching sound personality (chip-tune squeaks, wet clay splats, brass raspberries…). The mechanics don't change; only the feel does.
